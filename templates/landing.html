How We Built TactiRead

From an idea to a tactile math platform — our journey step by step.

Phase 01

Identifying the Problem

We began by interviewing teachers and students with visual impairments to understand their challenges in accessing math visuals. The main issue: graphs and charts were rarely tactile, leaving students unable to interpret mathematical relationships independently.

Phase 02

Prototyping the Tactile System

We moved from digital simulation to physical prototyping by fabricating a tactile module using a 3D printer and Arduino-based pin array. Each printed block and pin acted as a tactile unit that could rise or lower to represent data points on the graph, validating that curves can be experienced through touch.

Phase 03

Overcoming Technical Challenges

Mapping continuous functions to a 20×20 tactile grid was not straightforward — small changes in resolution caused major distortions. We solved this with adaptive sampling, boundary checks, and tactile-friendly rounding logic for each curve point.

Phase 04

Integrating with Arduino Grid

We connected our software to an Arduino-based 20×20 tactile pin matrix. Each plotted coordinate triggers a physical pin, letting blind users “feel” the curve directly — the moment abstract math became tangible.

Phase 05

Final Product & Testing

After refinement and feedback from ArtBlind and inclusive classrooms, TactiRead became a complete system — combining safe plotting, tactile output, and inclusive design. It now enables blind learners to explore math visually and by touch.

Final Showcase

Bringing Math to Life Through Touch

The final version of TactiRead transforms digital graphs into tactile experiences. Learners can trace functions by hand and perceive slopes, peaks, and intersections through physical feedback.

  • Real-time rendering: instantly sends plotted curves to the tactile grid.
  • Hardware integration: 3D-printed pin modules powered by Arduino logic.
  • Inclusive feedback loop: refined in collaboration with ArtBlind.
